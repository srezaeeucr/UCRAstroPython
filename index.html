Course Description

This intensive practical course is designed specifically for first-year PhD students in astronomy and astrophysics who need to master the computational tools that form the backbone of modern observational research. Whether your future research focuses on galaxy evolution, stellar astrophysics, cosmology, or exoplanets, you will inevitably encounter multi-wavelength imaging, spectroscopic analysis, and complex data reduction challenges that require sophisticated computational skills.

The course bridges the gap between undergraduate coursework and the research-grade analysis techniques you'll need throughout your PhD and beyond. Rather than focusing on theoretical concepts, we emphasize hands-on experience with the same tools, software packages, and methodologies used in cutting-edge astronomical research. You'll work with real observational data from major facilities like HST, Keck, VLT, and JWST, learning not just how to use these tools, but when and why to apply specific techniques.

By the end of this course, you'll have a comprehensive toolkit for tackling observational astronomy challenges: from reducing raw CCD images to extracting meaningful astrophysical parameters, from handling complex multi-dimensional datasets to creating publication-quality results. More importantly, you'll develop the problem-solving mindset and code development practices that will make you an effective computational researcher.

This course directly prepares you for the practical demands of modern astronomy research, where computational proficiency often determines the success and impact of your scientific investigations. The skills you develop here will serve as the foundation for your thesis research and your future career in astronomy.

Learning Outcomes

  • Master essential Python astronomy libraries and their optimal usage patterns.
  • Develop robust data reduction and analysis workflows for multi-wavelength observations.
  • Implement proper error propagation and uncertainty quantification throughout analysis pipelines.
  • Create reproducible, well-documented code suitable for publication and collaboration.
  • Critically evaluate analysis choices and systematic uncertainties in observational astronomy.
  • Collaborate using GitHub and code reviews.
